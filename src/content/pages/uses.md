## Writing
**Why I write in Markdown.** Writing in plain text guarantees absolute content longevity. Word processors and proprietary databases create vendor lock-in. Markdown files can be searched, compiled, and read on any system fifty years from now.

## Development
**Why I prefer simple tooling.** Complex configurations introduce subtle breakages. I favor terminal tools and minimal environments because they reduce the surface area of potential friction. If an editor requires more than a few lines of configuration to be usable, it is too complex.

## Learning
**Why I study from primary sources.** Tutorials summarize another person's understanding, which frequently filters out essential edge cases. Reading the raw RFCs, system documentation, and specifications reveals the actual trade-offs that designers struggled with.

## Reading
**How I annotate books.** Passive reading leaves little lasting trace. I read physical copies or static PDFs, writing summaries, margins arguments, and systems structures diagrams inside my notes for every chapter finished.

## Planning
**Why I keep plans lightweight.** Detailed long-term charts rarely survive contact with real-world requirements. I structure tasks in simple, text-based checklists that cover only immediate, actionable goals for the current development cycles.

## Research
**How I validate ideas.** Before writing code, I compile a system architecture model, mapping database constraints and request sequences. Only when the data states transition predictably do I begin writing source code.
